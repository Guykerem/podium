# GTD Essentials

Getting Things Done (GTD) is a personal productivity methodology developed by David Allen in his 2001 book *Getting Things Done: The Art of Stress-Free Productivity*. Its core insight: the mind is for having ideas, not holding them. Offload every commitment into a trusted external system so attention can go to the work itself.

## The Five Steps

1. **Capture** — Collect every open loop (tasks, ideas, commitments, worries) into inboxes. Paper, notes app, email, voice memo. The rule: if it occupies mental space, it gets captured.
2. **Clarify** — For each captured item, ask: *is it actionable?* If no → trash, reference, or someday/maybe. If yes → what's the next physical action?
3. **Organize** — Put the outcome somewhere you trust: calendar (time-specific), next-actions list (by context), projects list (multi-step outcomes), waiting-for (delegated), someday/maybe.
4. **Reflect** — Review the system. Daily: calendar and next-actions. Weekly: full review of all lists, projects, and horizons.
5. **Engage** — Work from trusted lists, choosing based on context, time available, energy, and priority.

## The 2-Minute Rule

If an action will take less than two minutes, do it immediately rather than capturing it. The overhead of tracking is greater than the cost of doing.

## Next-Action Thinking

The smallest concrete physical step that moves a project forward. "Plan birthday party" is a project; "text Sarah to ask about Saturday availability" is a next action. Vague items ("think about budget") stall. Specific ones ("open spreadsheet, add January expenses row") move.

## Projects vs. Tasks

- **Task (next action)** — single step. "Reply to Mira."
- **Project** — any desired outcome requiring more than one action. "Hire a designer" is a project; its next action might be "draft job description."

Every project should have at least one defined next action at all times. A project without a next action is where things die.

## The Weekly Review

The keystone habit. Without it, the system degrades and trust collapses. Allen's checklist:

- Collect loose papers and notes
- Process inboxes to zero
- Review calendar (past week for follow-ups, next two weeks for prep)
- Review waiting-for list
- Review projects list — does each have a next action?
- Review someday/maybe — anything ready to activate?
- Review goals and longer horizons (less frequent)

Budget 30-90 minutes. Protect it.

## Applicability to AI Agents

An assistant running GTD on behalf of a user is essentially maintaining the trusted system. The capture step happens through communication (email, chat, dictation). Clarification and organization become the assistant's core work. Reflection is the weekly review the assistant initiates. Engagement is choosing what to surface when.

The principle that maps most directly: **the mind is for having ideas, not holding them**. A good assistant absorbs the holding so the user can focus on having.
