# Email Etiquette

Email is asynchronous, persistent, and forwardable. Every email should be written with those three facts in mind.

## Subject Lines

Good subject lines let the recipient decide, in one glance, whether and when to open. Patterns that work:

- **Action + object** — "Review: Q2 budget draft"
- **Decision needed by date** — "Decision by Fri: vendor A or B"
- **Status tag** — "FYI:", "Action:", "Heads up:"
- **Specific noun** — "Tuesday's interview — logistics"

Avoid: "Quick question", "Following up", "Hi", "Hey", blank subjects.

## BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

Lead with the point. Context and reasoning go after, not before. A reader on mobile should learn the most important thing from the first two lines of the preview.

Before: "Hope your week is going well. I've been thinking about our conversation last Tuesday and I had a chance to look at the numbers again and I think we should probably consider pushing the deadline."
After: "I think we should push the deadline two weeks. Reasoning below."

## CC and BCC

- **To** — people you need a response from.
- **CC** — people who need to be aware but not to act. If you CC everyone, nobody acts.
- **BCC** — for large distribution (announcements, newsletters) to protect addresses, or to silently drop someone off a thread ("moving X to BCC" is a common polite move).

Rule of thumb: if the CC list grows past five, reconsider whether email is the right medium.

## Response-Time Expectations

Rough norms (adjust to your industry):

- **Internal peer** — same day, often within a few hours
- **Manager / senior** — same day if possible, next morning acceptable
- **Direct report** — same day; faster if they're blocked
- **External client** — within one business day
- **Cold outreach** — no obligation; responding within a week is polite

Silence past 48 hours on an internal thread usually means someone needs a nudge.

## Assume It Will Be Forwarded

Every email may end up in front of someone you didn't send it to — a manager, a lawyer, a reporter, a future you. Consequences:

- No snark about third parties
- No confidential data without explicit trust
- No venting
- Write the line you'd be comfortable reading aloud

## When NOT to Email

- **It's urgent** — call, text, or walk over
- **It's delicate** — voice or video handles tone better
- **It's more than three back-and-forths** — switch to a call
- **It's a decision requiring discussion** — meet
- **It involves strong emotion** — wait, then choose a richer medium

Email is for asynchronous record. If either asynchrony or record is unwanted, pick a different channel.

## Small Conventions That Matter

- **Signatures** — Name, role, one way to reach you. Skip the quotes and the four social icons.
- **Replies** — "Reply all" only when all genuinely need it.
- **Attachments** — Mention them in the body; name files humanly ("Q2-budget-2026-04-17.pdf", not "final_FINAL_v3.pdf").
- **Opening lines** — Skip "I hope this email finds you well" unless you know each other; it reads as filler.
- **Closing** — Match the register. "Thanks," works for almost everything. "Best," for slightly more formal. "Sincerely," for truly formal.

## One Email, One Point

If an email has three unrelated asks, the recipient will answer the easiest and forget the other two. Send three emails, or one email with a numbered list and explicit asks.
