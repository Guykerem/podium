# Professional Tone

Tone is the emotional register of a message — warm, neutral, formal, casual, firm. It's distinct from content: the same information can land very differently depending on tone. For an assistant drafting on someone's behalf, tone is the primary site of error — wrong content is rare, wrong tone is common.

## The Three Variables

Tone adjusts along three dimensions:

1. **Relationship** — who is the other person to the user?
2. **Channel** — what medium carries the message?
3. **Stakes** — how sensitive is the moment?

## By Relationship

- **Peer / close collaborator** — Warm, direct, low ceremony. Contractions fine. First names.
- **Manager / senior** — Respectful, concise, solution-oriented. Lead with the ask or update, not apologies.
- **Direct report** — Clear, encouraging, specific. Praise is specific; criticism is about the work.
- **External (client, vendor, stranger)** — Slightly more formal. Full sentences. Title + last name until invited otherwise. No inside jokes.
- **Cold outreach** — Shortest respectful version. Why you, why them, the ask, one line.

## By Channel

- **Email** — Complete sentences, clear subject, signature. Assume it may be forwarded.
- **Slack / chat** — Shorter, conversational. Emoji/reactions appropriate with peers, cautious upward.
- **DM / SMS** — Shortest. Skip greetings unless re-opening after silence.
- **Letter / formal memo** — Full register. Salutation and sign-off matter.

## By Stakes

- **Routine** — Default register for the relationship.
- **Delicate** (declining, correcting, delivering bad news) — Soften the frame, harden the content. Be kind *and* unambiguous. Don't bury the point in pleasantries.
- **High-stakes** (legal, HR, conflict) — Neutral, factual, short. Nothing off-the-cuff. Preserve record.

## Before/After Examples

**Request to a peer (Slack)**
- Before: "Hi, I hope you're doing well. I was wondering if you might have a moment to possibly take a look at the draft when you get a chance."
- After: "Hey — mind taking a look at the draft when you get a minute?"

**Declining an external request (email)**
- Before: "Unfortunately, due to various circumstances, we will be unable to move forward at this time, though we very much appreciate the opportunity."
- After: "Thanks for thinking of us. We can't take this on right now, but I'd be glad to reconnect in Q3."

**Giving feedback to a report**
- Before: "I think maybe the intro could possibly be a bit stronger if you wanted to revise it."
- After: "The intro buries the main point. Try leading with the result in the first sentence, then the method."

**Delivering bad news to a manager**
- Before: "I'm so sorry to bother you, there's been a bit of a setback and I wanted to flag it in case it might be relevant."
- After: "Heads up: the vendor delay pushes launch by a week. Options below."

## Drafting Principles

- **Match, then nudge.** Mirror the recipient's register by one click; don't out-formal or out-casual them.
- **Read it as them.** Before sending, read the draft in the recipient's voice. Does anything sting, confuse, or condescend?
- **Warmth is not padding.** One genuine human line beats three hedging ones.
- **Brevity respects.** A short message to a busy person is a kindness, not a coldness.
