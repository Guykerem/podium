# Time Blocking

Time blocking is the practice of assigning every hour of the day a purpose in advance, on the calendar. It turns a todo list (infinite, flat) into a schedule (bounded, prioritized). Proponents include Cal Newport, who ties it to deep work, and Nir Eyal, who frames it as "turning your values into time."

## The Core Move

Instead of "I'll work on the report today," the calendar says "9:00-10:30: draft report introduction." The block names the work, the time, and (implicitly) what won't happen during that time.

## Deep Work vs. Shallow Work

From Cal Newport's *Deep Work* (2016):

- **Deep work** — Cognitively demanding tasks performed in a state of distraction-free concentration. Writes the report, designs the system, writes the proof. Scarce and valuable.
- **Shallow work** — Logistical, reactive, non-cognitively-demanding tasks. Email, scheduling, status updates. Necessary but not leveraged.

Time blocking protects deep work by making it visible and defended. A good day has 2-4 hours of deep blocks before the shallow tide rises.

## Themed Days

Assign whole days a dominant mode. E.g.:

- **Maker days** — long deep blocks, no meetings
- **Manager days** — back-to-back calls, 1:1s, decisions
- **Admin days** — inbox, expenses, planning

Reduces context-switching cost. Popularized by Jack Dorsey (Twitter/Square) and Paul Graham's "maker's schedule vs. manager's schedule" essay.

## Calendar as Intention, Not Inbox

A calendar filled only with meetings someone else booked shows what others want from you. A calendar filled also with your own blocks shows what you want from yourself. Time blocking is the practice of making the second set visible.

## Buffer Time

Meetings rarely end on time. Deep work rarely starts on time. Transitions cost 5-15 minutes. Solutions:

- Default meetings to 25 or 50 minutes instead of 30/60
- Insert 15-minute buffers between back-to-back calls
- Put one "open block" per day for slippage, drop-ins, and recovery

Without buffers, the schedule is brittle; one late meeting cascades through the day.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Over-packing** — scheduling every minute assumes no interruptions, no thinking time, no bathroom breaks. Budget 70-80% of the workday, not 100%.
- **Ignoring energy** — a deep block at 3 PM for someone whose peak is 9 AM is a block that won't happen. See `energy-management.md`.
- **Treating blocks as sacred** — the point is intention, not rigidity. When reality shifts, re-block. A missed block is information, not a moral failure.
- **Blocking only work** — lunch, walks, transitions, and rest deserve blocks too. Otherwise they get eaten.
- **Planning the day at the start of the day** — by then, the reactive tide has hit. Block tomorrow at the end of today, or block the week on Sunday.

## For an Assistant

A time-blocking assistant does three things well:
1. Proposes the next day's blocks at end-of-day, informed by commitments and priorities.
2. Guards deep blocks against meeting requests that could land elsewhere.
3. Re-blocks in real time when reality diverges — without making it feel like failure.
