# Energy Management

Time is finite and uniform; energy is finite and variable. Two hours at peak are not equivalent to two hours at trough. Energy management argues that scheduling by time alone underfits reality — the right task at the wrong energy is wasted on both ends.

## The Loehr & Schwartz Framework

From Jim Loehr and Tony Schwartz, *The Power of Full Engagement* (2003). They identify four interdependent sources of energy:

- **Physical** — sleep, nutrition, movement, hydration. The foundation; if this is depleted, the others collapse.
- **Emotional** — the quality of feeling. Positive, connected, secure states expand capacity; anxious, defensive, angry states shrink it.
- **Mental** — focus, clarity, creative capacity. Depleted by multitasking and decision fatigue; restored by single-tasking and rest.
- **Spiritual** — sense of purpose and alignment with values. The deepest reservoir; fuels the others when the work matters.

The prescription: manage energy as deliberately as time. Oscillate between expenditure and renewal, across all four dimensions.

## Ultradian Rhythms

The body cycles through roughly 90-minute peaks of alertness followed by 20-minute troughs of recovery need — a pattern identified by Nathaniel Kleitman in the 1960s and extended to waking hours. Signs a cycle is ending: yawning, restlessness, hunger, difficulty focusing, craving sugar or social contact.

Practical implication: ~90-minute work blocks followed by real breaks outperform 4-hour grinds. The break isn't laziness; it's the second half of the cycle.

## Peak vs. Trough — Matching Tasks

A useful crude map:

- **Peak energy** — creative work, hard writing, strategy, difficult conversations, anything novel or cognitively demanding. Don't waste peaks on email.
- **Mid-range** — meetings, routine analysis, collaborative work, decisions with low novelty.
- **Trough** — administrative work, inbox triage, expense reports, light reading, walking meetings. Tasks where low energy is survivable or even useful.

For most people, peak sits in the first 2-3 hours after waking (after a short warm-up). A second, smaller peak often appears in late afternoon. The post-lunch dip (1-3 PM) is near-universal.

## Chronotype Variation

Roughly 25% of people are morning types, 25% evening types, 50% intermediate (per work by Till Roenneburg). A morning type's peak-task schedule is a reversed evening type's. An assistant must not assume the 9 AM-is-peak default.

## Eliciting a User's Energy Map

Good questions:

- "When in the day do you feel sharpest? When does your energy reliably dip?"
- "What kind of work lights you up? What drains you, even when you're good at it?"
- "When did you last do your best work of the week? What was the setup?"
- "Which meetings leave you energized? Which leave you depleted?"
- "How many hours of true focus work can you do in a day before diminishing returns?"

Treat the answers as hypotheses to test, not facts. Revisit after a week of observation: did the schedule match the felt experience?

## For an Assistant

Three practical moves:
1. Protect the peak. Don't fill it with meetings the user would accept by default.
2. Match task type to energy window when scheduling, not just to calendar availability.
3. Notice drift. If the user consistently skips morning deep blocks, the map may be wrong — revisit.
