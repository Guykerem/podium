# Relationship Cadence

A personal CRM treats relationships the way a business CRM treats customers: explicit, tracked, tended with intention. The goal isn't to industrialize friendship. It's to stop accidentally losing people who matter to you.

## Dunbar Layers

Robin Dunbar's research identifies stable, nested layers in personal networks, each roughly three times larger than the last:

- **~5** — Intimate support clique. Closest friends, family, partner. The people you'd call at 3 AM.
- **~15** — Sympathy group. Close friends you see often and confide in.
- **~50** — Good friends. Regular social contact; invited to life events.
- **~150** — Meaningful connections (the famous "Dunbar's number"). People whose name, face, and story you know.
- **~500 / 1500** — Acquaintances and recognizable faces. Weak ties.

The layers aren't aspirations; they're capacity limits. You can't move everyone to the inner layer. You can choose who sits where, and tend accordingly.

## Cadence Targets by Layer

These are defaults, not prescriptions — adjust to the relationship and culture. A reasonable starting map:

- **5 (inner)** — meaningful contact weekly or more. Drift past two weeks is a signal.
- **15** — monthly contact. Drift past two months is a signal.
- **50** — quarterly contact. Drift past six months is a signal.
- **150** — yearly touch (birthday, holiday, life event). Drift past 18 months is a signal.
- **Beyond 150** — opportunistic. No obligation of cadence.

"Contact" can be asynchronous — a text, a voice note, a shared article with a real comment. It doesn't have to be a long call.

## Signs of Drift

- You think of them and realize you can't remember the last time you spoke.
- You learn something major about their life from a third party or social media.
- They initiate more than you do, consistently, for months.
- You feel a small prick of guilt when their name comes up.
- Their life has changed (job, move, loss, birth) and you didn't acknowledge it.

Drift is recoverable. Ignored drift calcifies into estrangement.

## Warm Re-Entry Patterns

When the last contact is distant, the instinct is often to apologize, over-explain, or ghost further. Better patterns:

- **The no-ask check-in** — "Thought of you today because of X. No need to reply — just wanted you to know."
- **The shared-context opener** — reference something specific you know about them, not a generic "how are you."
- **The acknowledgment without apology spiral** — "It's been too long" is fine. Three paragraphs about why isn't.

See `warm-reconnect.md` for specific openers.

## Staying in Touch vs. Transactional Networking

The difference is usually obvious to the recipient:

- **Staying in touch** — contact is its own point. You don't need anything. Interest in their life is sincere.
- **Transactional networking** — contact is a means. The ask is in the second message, or implied from the first.

Both have their place; conflating them breeds resentment. A good assistant helps the user do the first often enough that the occasional second doesn't feel mercenary.

## For an Assistant

Concrete work:
1. Maintain a layered list with last-contact dates and cadence targets.
2. Surface drift early, not late — a nudge at month 5 for a quarterly contact, not month 12.
3. Notice life events (moves, jobs, birthdays, losses) and prompt acknowledgment.
4. Never auto-send. Drafts and prompts, yes. Robotic check-ins from your name, no.
