# Warm Reconnect

How to message someone you haven't talked to in a long time without making it weird. The failure modes are well-known: over-apologizing, over-explaining, fake enthusiasm, transactional openers, or ghosting further because the guilt compounded.

## Why It Feels Hard

The silence accrues meaning in your head that the other person almost certainly isn't tracking. They rarely feel abandoned; they feel busy, like you. The weight of the gap is usually one-sided — yours. That asymmetry is the first thing to correct for when writing.

## Core Principles

- **Short beats long.** A four-line message that lands is worth more than a long one that tries to justify itself.
- **Specific beats generic.** "How are you?" is a nothing-opener. A reference to something specific signals you remember them as a person.
- **No apology spiral.** Acknowledge the gap in one line at most. Long apologies make the recipient feel obligated to reassure you, which inverts the kindness.
- **No ask on first re-contact.** Especially after a long silence. If you need something, re-establish first, then ask in a second message days or weeks later.
- **Warmth, not pressure.** "No need to reply" is generous. "It would mean a lot to hear back" is obligation.

## The Pattern Interrupt Opener

The risk of a standard "hey, long time" message is that it slots into the "I'll reply later" mental bucket and is forgotten. A pattern interrupt — a concrete, specific, sensory detail — breaks the auto-reply reflex. It reminds the reader they're talking to a specific person, not a generic contact.

Structure:
1. **Trigger** — the specific thing that made you think of them (a song, a place, an article, a memory)
2. **Brief acknowledgment of the gap** — one line, no spiral
3. **Small offer or open door** — no ask, no pressure

## Acknowledging the Gap

Options, in order of brevity:

- "Long time." (four characters past "hi")
- "It's been a minute."
- "I know it's been ages — thinking of you."
- "Been meaning to write forever. Finally am."

Avoid: "I'm so sorry I've been such a bad friend, life has been crazy, I know that's not an excuse..." This makes the recipient the comforter. Don't.

## Example Openers

**1. The song/place trigger**

> Heard Blackbird on the radio this morning and it took me straight back to that drive to Mendocino. Hope you're well — no need to reply, just wanted to say I was thinking of you.

**2. The article forward with context**

> Read this and immediately thought of our conversation about architecture — [link]. It's been too long. Would love to catch up whenever you surface.

**3. The life-event acknowledgment**

> Just saw you started at Figma — congratulations, that's a great fit. It's been a while; hope the transition is going well. Drink sometime this spring?

**4. The low-stakes open door**

> I know I've been a ghost. Nothing urgent — just want you to know you've been on my mind. If you're ever up for a coffee, I'm in.

## If They Don't Reply

Silence isn't rejection. People miss messages, get buried, mean to reply, don't. One unanswered reconnect is not grounds for a second round of guilt. Wait a few months; try a different angle; or accept the relationship has moved to a lighter layer. That's allowed.

## For an Assistant

Drafting a warm reconnect means:
1. Pulling a real detail from prior context (where available) — a shared trip, project, interest, or last conversation topic.
2. Keeping the draft short — the user can lengthen if they want; they rarely shorten.
3. Flagging when a reconnect should probably be voice or in-person instead of text (very close past relationships, major life events).
4. Never, under any circumstances, auto-sending. This is the user's voice, and silence is sometimes the right answer.
