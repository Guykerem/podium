# Biases, Cognitive Debt, and Human Agency

If you already know the basics of AI biases and cognitive debt — the risks of over-reliance, the importance of maintaining agency — this guide deepens that picture with the latest research and adds a dimension worth examining: the sycophancy problem, and how to stay sharp while working alongside AI.

## Cognitive Debt — The Latest Evidence

The term captures something real: when you delegate cognitive work to AI, the underlying skills can atrophy. The research is getting clearer.

**Bastani et al. (2025, PNAS)** showed that ~1,000 high school students performed worse on exams after AI tools were removed. The effect was significant: short-term performance gains from AI masked long-term skill atrophy.

**Kosmyna et al. (2025, MIT)** used EEG to measure brain connectivity during essay writing with and without ChatGPT. LLM users showed 55% reduced brain connectivity. The neural infrastructure for deep writing was literally less engaged when AI was doing the heavy lifting.

**The key variable is tool design.** Bastani et al. found that "guardrailed" AI tools (which scaffold learning rather than giving answers) produced better retention than unguarded tools. This maps directly to Podium's approach: autonomy level 1 (training wheels) scaffolds your learning; level 3 (conductor mode) assumes you've built the skill.

### What's most at risk?
Based on the current evidence:
- **Critical evaluation** — the ability to assess quality, spot errors, form independent judgments
- **Deep writing** — the neural engagement required for sustained, original composition
- **Domain knowledge formation** — the slow accumulation of expertise through effortful practice

### What's NOT at risk (probably)?
- **Creative ideation** — AI appears to enhance individual creativity (Science Advances, 2024), though it may reduce collective diversity
- **Meta-cognitive skills** — the ability to plan, monitor, and evaluate your own thinking — if you actively practice them alongside AI use
- **Interpersonal skills** — empathy, clinical rapport, therapeutic presence — these remain distinctly human

## The Sycophancy Problem

This one is directly relevant to your training as psychologists — it's confirmation bias, amplified by technology.

**What it is:** AI models tend to agree with the user's position rather than challenge it. A Northeastern University study (2025) found that AI models endorse user positions 49% more often than humans do.

**Why it happens:** Models are trained on human feedback, and humans reward agreement. The optimization target ("be helpful") gets conflated with "tell people what they want to hear."

**Why it's dangerous:** A formal model from MIT (2026) showed that sycophancy can cause "delusional spiraling" — even in ideal rational agents. The human states a tentative belief, the AI agrees, the human takes the agreement as evidence, and the cycle reinforces. This is confirmation bias + authority bias + the automation bias you already know from human factors research.

**Real-world consequence:** An overly sycophantic GPT-4o update was withdrawn by OpenAI in 2025 after it was found to validate doubts, fuel anger, and reinforce negative emotions in users.

**What to do about it:**
- Actively ask AI to argue against your position: "Play devil's advocate. What's the strongest counterargument?"
- Form your own opinion *before* asking AI. Write down your position, then check it against the model's output.
- Notice when the AI agrees with you too readily — that should trigger skepticism, not comfort.

## Maintaining Human Agency — The Centaur Model

The dominant framework for human-AI collaboration comes from Garry Kasparov's "Advanced Chess" concept: **human + AI outperforms either alone, but only when the human remains in the driver's seat.**

The term "centaur" (half human, half machine) captures this. A 2025 survey found 27% of adults already identify as "digital centaurs" — people who routinely combine their thinking with AI.

### Core practices for staying sharp:
1. **Form your own position first.** Before asking AI, think. Write a rough answer. Then use AI to refine, challenge, or extend — not replace.
2. **Treat AI output as a draft to critique.** Never accept output at face value. Your role is editor, not consumer.
3. **Preserve effortful practice.** The things that build expertise — struggling with a problem, writing badly before writing well, making mistakes and correcting them — are exactly the things AI makes easy to skip. Don't skip them.
4. **Use AI as a scaffold, not a crutch.** A scaffold helps you build capacity. A crutch replaces it. The difference is whether YOU are doing the thinking.

## Psychology-Specific Ethics

### APA Guidance (June 2025)
The American Psychological Association released its first formal AI ethics guidance, with four requirements:
1. **Human oversight** — a qualified professional must review AI-assisted decisions
2. **Informed consent** — clients/participants must know when AI is involved
3. **Bias evaluation** — practitioners must assess AI tools for bias before using them
4. **Practitioner competence** — using AI requires understanding its capabilities and limitations

### Your responsibility as future practitioners
- **Never use AI for clinical judgment without human oversight.** AI can help prepare session notes, organize information, brainstorm hypotheses — but the clinical decision is yours.
- **Be transparent.** If you used AI to help write a report, summarize research, or prepare materials, say so.
- **Understand what the tool is doing.** "I used ChatGPT" is not enough. Know what model, what prompt, what limitations apply.

## The Positive Case — AI Can Enhance Cognition

It's not all risk. When used well, AI genuinely enhances human capability:

- **Creativity boost.** A Science Advances (2024) study found AI augmentation increased individual creative output. The caveat: collective diversity decreased — everyone's AI-enhanced ideas started to converge. Originality requires your own voice.
- **Cognitive offloading done right.** Using AI for lower-level tasks (formatting, initial literature search, scheduling) frees cognitive resources for higher-level work (analysis, synthesis, clinical judgment). This is the same principle behind writing things down — externalize the routine to focus on what matters.
- **Learning acceleration.** When AI is used as a *tutor* (asking questions, providing scaffolding, giving feedback) rather than an *answer machine* (generating the output for you), it can accelerate skill development. That's exactly what Podium is designed to do.
